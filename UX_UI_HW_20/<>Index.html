A Family for Every Child (AFFEC) Website Redesign

Redesigned Mobile & Desktop homepages

Project Dates & Scope

3-weeks in December 2021; discovery, branding, interface design, and communication strategy services

Team & Roles

A collaborative effort between 4 UX/UI design and research students. My role focused on the definition & ideation phases.

Tools

Figma, Miro, Slack, Adobe XD, Google Slides, Google Sheets, Zoom, Invision

Overview

We were tasked with developing a responsive Website Redesign for a Nonprofit website. We choose to work with A Family For Every Child (AFFEC), an adoption agency “dedicated to finding loving, permanent families for every waiting foster child.”

Our goal was to amplify the mission of AFFEC by redesigning its website for improved client usability and engagement through the UX/UI research and design process.

Our work resulted in organized and professional adoption agency website landing page to help prospective adoptive parents become competent and well-informed about the adoption process.

User Research & Defining the Problem

Empathize

To understand our user's experience of adopting a foster child, we needed to jump into their shoes.

We created a research plan and conducted 5 qualitative interviews with individuals who have had varying experiences with foster adoption. We gathered observations and organized them in an affinity diagram to discover patterns and insights.

We also aquired valuable insights from AFFEC’s Executive Director, who helped us understand theagency’ services and further diagnose the website’s problems.

Last, we identified industry competitors to assess AFFEC's brand’s strengths and weaknesses.

Define

We analyzed our data using an empathy map and conducted a competitor SWOT analysis. We also analyzed the website’s interface using a heuristics analysis . These efforts led us to 3 key insights and allowed us to uncover the problem and possible solution for our case study.

Key Insights

  1. AFFEC's largest clientele demographic was prospective adoptive parents, but the website was not targeting and attracting them.
  2. AFFEC lagged with its UX/UI usability heuristics compared to its competitors. Most significantly, it communicated a childish brand that did not portray professionalism and expertise.
  3. AFFEC clients fail to get involved with the adoption process because they assume it is logistaically complex and emotionally and challenging.

Obstacles

Clients fail to get involved with the adoption process because they assume it is logistaically complex and emotionally challenging.

Positive Aspects

There has been an increase in adoption since the pandemic. Clients are seeking to make well-informed and intentional adoptions.

To Improve

Communicate a professional & trustworthy brand, guide new clients with structured onboarding, improver overall usabilty and acessibiilty huristics

Problem

When prospective adoptive parents don't have good information about the adoption process, decisions can be made based on fear and stereotypes, which can deter good foster parents and leave others ill-prepared.

Solution

By reorganizing and rebranding A Family For Every Child's website to demonstrate trustworthiness, knowledge, and client support, potential parents will be better prepared to pursue and complete the adoption process.

Ideation and Brainstorming Solutions

Ideate

To design successful UX esperiences for AFFEC's clients, we needed to futher understand how they interact with website. I conceptualized a user journey map with UX scenarios and user personas and visualized it in a storyboard. I wanted to understand how prospective parents with diverging perspectives would interact with AFFEC's website from a relationship context.

My UX scenario revealed the need to design for clients with a wide range of emotional states and viewpoints. With this insight, we prioritized a calming and neutral interface where clients feel at ease and when accessing content.

To further improve client's access to contnet, we deployed a card sorting strategy and user flow diagrams to organize the information architecture and identify the routes that users take when navigating the website. Through these methods, we conceptualized three new features to prototype and test.

New homepage card modules welcome and guide clients to information and resources relevant to their specific needs and interests.
New Site Map with reconstructed information architecture for easy above the fold naviagation

Proposed Features for Improvement

  1. Homepage card modules with identified user flows
  2. An all-inclusive navigation bar located above the fold
  3. Enhanced call to action buttons (donate, view adaptable kids, and family login)

Feature Iterations & Prototyping

Prototype

We built out our new features using wireframe prototypes populated them with UI elements inspired by a moodboard. We ensured design consistency by creating a UI style guide with standard UI elements and interactions.

Aesthetic Objectives

  1. Enhance AFFEC’s simple, memorable, and impactful original logo design
  2. Incorporate handmade and home-made style and imagery
  3. Implement a 'Heartwarming red’ and 'calming blue' color palette

Interactive UI Elements

  1. Focus and hover states on buttons and navigation bar
  2. Expanding hompeage card modules

Test

    We planned, conducted, and analyzed 3 phases of usability testing and prioritized feedback.

    Phase 1: Five-second usability tests on lo-fi desktop Prototype

    1. Place AFFEC’s mission below card modules to make the flows more apparent
    2. Ensure language is relatable to new users
    3. feature spotlight success stories for narrative

    Phase 2: User task Flows on mid-fi desktop prototype

    We created a testing plan to uncover how users navigated our card module user flows. Results revealed that the cards were successful navigation features, resulting in a 100% Completion rate.

    Phase 3: A/B testing on mid-fi prototypes homepages

    Mobile: Users preferred a consistent hero image across devices with solid card modules located below AFFEC’s Mission statement.

    Desktop: Users favored san serif typography, transparent interactive cards, and a navy color for the navigation bar.
